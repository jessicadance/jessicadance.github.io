I have decided to present this timeline on the relationship between dance and computer-based technology. I initially planned to cover the broader topic of “dance and technology”, covering a large span of history, from the era of gas-lighting innovations to the advanced technologies of today. However, during my research I became increasingly interested in specifically computer-based technologies. Therefore I have concentrated my timeline on the age of the computer, from 1946 to today. The term “computer-based technology” covers not only innovations such as the CD-ROM, choreographic software, and specific uses of the internet, but also those such as motion-capture technologies and Virtual Reality.

It seemed fitting to present my timeline as a website, which I have built from scratch. It was a daunting challenge, but in doing so, the process of creating this timeline became an interaction with the very area history I have chosen to present. Where possible, I have provided images, videos, and links so that you may experience some of the creations being described for yourself.

I hope that you enjoy this rather specific journey through history. By looking at events in this way, we can see how the early pioneers who ventured to bring computer technologies and dance together laid the foundations for the incredible innovations we are seeing today. Perhaps you, like me, will wonder at all the possibilities of where the collaboration between dance and computer technology will lead to next.

EARLY COMPUTER TECHNOLOGIES

1946 ‘The first general-purpose electronic computer was completed, ENIAC (Electronic Numerical Integrator and Calculator)… the ENIAC had to be rewired for each problem or task’ (Smith, 1992, p.52).



Photograph of the ENIAC, from US Information Agency.


1949 The first operational stored-program electrical computer was invented at the University of Cambridge, ‘bringing about the development of digital computers in their present form’ (Smith, 1992, p. 52).

1960s

1964 Collaborators at the University of Pittsburgh created a dance using an IBM 7070 computer to generate sets of instructions for solo performers. ‘The human-machine interaction for dance was initiated’ (Smith, 1992, p. 53).

1965 The first commercial minicomputer, the predecessor for the kind used today, was installed (as opposed to previous computers, which required their own air-conditioned room) (Smith, 1992).

1966 A. Michael Noll created 'one of the earliest computer-assisted dances’ using a program which indicated stage positions and body facings using very basic stick figures (Smith, 1992, p.55). ‘Noll used an electronic pencil and a plate as an inputting device, and created a four-minute dance preserved on 16-mm film using a camera shooting a picture of the screen step-by-step’ (Smith, 1992, p. 55).

1969 Twyla Tharp choreographed History of the Up and Down, which used a computer to select movements from a list of elements, and also chose other technical aspects, such as lighting (Smith, 1992). Video available here!

1969 Carol Withrow created a graphics program to simulate a moving figure ‘as a prototype for a choreographer’s tool. The program allowed the operator to control movements of only two moving joints (right hip and right knee) on a three-dimensional stick figure’ (Smith, 1992, p. 56).

1970s

1971 Merce Cunningham created Loops as a solo dance. This would later be recorded, re-worked and re-imagined in a variety of ways with new technology (Kaiser, in Bleeker, 2017).


A sample of Merce Cunningham's Loops.

1980s

1986 Tom Calvert began researching LifeForms, then called Compose, ‘a computer tool to aid dancers and choreographers in the interactive design and specification of human movement’ (Armin, in Smith, 1992, p. 11).

1989 Plans began to produce a CD-ROM resource containing terminology used within the five main schools of classical ballet (Ryman, Beatty and Roes, in Smith, 1995).


Ryman, Beatty and Roes' Dictionary-Database of Classical Ballet Terminology (in Smith, 1995, p.52).


1990s

1991 LifeForms 1.0 released, the popular human movement simulation software (Gee and Marchant, in Smith, 1995).

1991 A program was developed to produce signals based on what is seen by a video camera. This led to the development of The Virtual Stage Environment (See 1995) (Lovell and Mitchell, in Smith, 1995).

1991 The KACOR (Kinetic Audiological Communication Research) Group in Stockholm developed MOVE I ‘a movement description language that is used together with the animation program Anima II’ for ‘symbolic, textual and graphical representation of human movement’ (Magnus, in Smith, 1992, p. 29).

1992 Artist Paul Sermon presented the work Telematic Dreaming, at an exhibition of contemporary art in Amsterdam. ‘It involved two beds, one in the gallery with the viewers and one at a remote location. A ‘performer’ lay on the bed in the remote location and his or her image was projected into the gallery. Cameras and television screens allowed the performer to see his or her body interacting with the viewers, as they touched the bed’ (Popat, 2006, p. 53- 54).

Telematic Dreaming by Paul Sermon (1993) from V2_ on Vimeo

1992 The first annual Dance and Technology conference took place at the University of Wisconsin-Madison (Smith, 1992).

1992 Margo K Apostolos presented a session at the first annual Dance and Technology conference featuring examples of robot choreography, shown on slides and VHS (Apostolos, in Smith, 1992).

1994 Musae Dance was founded from a group of undergraduate and graduate University of Michigan dancers, with the purpose of exploring ‘the choreographic capabilities of the latest Macintosh LifeForms software’ (Gee and Marchant, in Smith, 1995, p. 9).

1994 Plans began to catalogue an index of images related to dance, theatre and music at the Metropolitan Museum of Art, to create the automated Index of the Performing Arts (IPA) (Gelernter and Old, in Smith, 1995).



An example from Metropolitan Museum of Art's Index of the Performing Arts (in Smith, 1995, p. 13).

1995 The Virtual Stage Environment: Computer scientist and dancer Robb Lovell, and composer John Mitchell, conducted research into the possibility of the digitization of human movement in real-time in performance settings, and using this movement to control elements of a stage environment (Lovell and Mitchell, in Smith, 1995).

1995 William Forsythe and ZKM Karlsruhe worked with Frankfurt Ballett to create the first ‘digital dance school’, Self Meant to Govern (Ziegler, in Bleeker, 2017).

1996 Richard Lord created the work Progressive 2, featuring video clips showing different sections of the same dance ‘in a variety of fragmented arrangements, so that the dance does not flow smoothly… a comment on the nature of web-based work at the time when most people were still accessing the web via modems, with their narrow bandwidth requiring the compression of data to the minimum size’ (Popat, 2006, p. 50-51).

1997 Richard Lord created two more web-based works: Lifeblood, a dance which drew ‘on the idea of the ‘virtual’ dancer having no physical presence at all’ and existed only as a text description of what the dance would look like (Popat, 2006, p. 49); and Brownian Motion, a ‘web dance’ in which ‘the user can click on little gold men and little blue armchairs to make them move on a black screen. Different key combinations attract the men and armchairs to move towards to cursor, or dispel them around the screen’ (Popat, 2006, p.50). Although it no longer exists, you can try a simulation based on the same technology by clicking here!

1997 The Cassandra Project brought artists together using the web through ‘online jamming sessions to prepare for real-time videoconference performances with dancers, actors, musicians and artists across America and Canada’ (Popat, 2006, p. 54).

1998 Hand-Drawn Spaces: an installation based on a motion capture project between Merce Cunningham, Paul Kaiser and Shelley Eshkar (Bleeker, 2017).

1999 William Forsythe worked with Frankfurt Ballett and ZKM Karlsruhe to publish the CD-ROM William Forsythe: Improvisation Technologies. A Tool for the Analytical Dance Eye. (Ziefler, in Bleeker, 2017)


Improvisation Technologies, A Tool for the Analytical Dance Eye from UNSW iCinema Centre on Vimeo

1999 The International Dance and Technology 99 Conference took place at Arizona State University. Click here to read a New York Times article about the event.

2000s

2000 Australian group Company in Space created Escape Velocity which ‘used an ISDN link to create a virtual space where two dancers could interact.’ One dancer was in America, and the other in Australia; a cameraperson in each location filmed the dancer from within the space. ‘The video from each camera was combined and augmented with with colours and images via real-time video mixing and processing, and the resulting digitized performance was projected into the spaces alongside the dancers at each location’ (Popat, 2006, p. 54).

2001 A version of Merce Cunningham’s Loops was commissioned by the M.I.T. Media Lab for the ID/ Entity show, using motion-capture techniques, with Paul Kaiser and Shelly Eshkar (Kaiser, in Bleeker, 2017).

2003-2004 Choreography and Cognition: a collaborative research project between cognitive science and choreography with the Wayne McGregor dance company, and five scientists from different backgrounds, which laid the groundwork for working with artificial intelligence and related areas in choreography (Bleeker, 2017).

2004 EyeToy: AntiGrav is released, designed by Harmonix, one of the first games in which the player could use their own body to control the game (Harmonix, 2018).

2005 The sound score for Cunningham’s, Kaiser’s and Eshkar’s Loops was remade for Ars Electronica (Kaiser, in Bleeker, 2017). Learn more about Loops.

2005 YouTube was created (Fitzpatrick, 2010).


2006 The Nintendo Wii was released, a videogame console with a handheld controller which responds to movement (Nintendo, 2018).

2007 Loops was re-created in tryptich form, and its underlying code was released as open source. (Kaiser, in Bleeker, 2017)

2007 The Dance-tech project is created: “the social network for innovators in motion” (Dance-Tech, 2018). Dance-tech.net

2008 Contredanse Editions (Brussels) released the four-hour DVD-ROM Material for the Spine: A Movement Study with Steve Paxman. The DVD-ROM contained ‘audiovisual essays, motion capture sequences, and extracts from letter, classes and performances that together present an extensive introduction to Paxman’s modes of working and thinking’ (Corin, in Bleeker, 2017, p.32).

2009 Synchronous Objects was published online- ‘a collaborative choreographic visualisation project that flows from a dance to data to visual objects and back around again’, based on William Forsythe’s One Flat Thing, reproduced (Shaw, in Bleeker, 2017, p. 98). Click here to experience Synchronous Objects.

2009 Siobhan Davies Replay is launched, providing ‘access to multi-media content (video, text, audio, image) as well as a comprehensive collection of materials that document the creative dance-making process’ and was ‘the first digital archive of its kind in the UK’ (Whatley, in Bleeker, 2017, p.62).

2009 Just Dance game for Nintendo Wii is released (Nintendo, 2018).

2010- Today

2010 Whatever Dance Toolbox is created: ‘a product of the long-standing collaboration between the performance collective BADco. and German human-machine interface developer Daniel Turing’ which is ‘A suite of tools designed for analysis and development of dance and movement… a set of real-time video analyses which can work as an interactive mirror by displaying various transformations of real-time video image and/ or visualizations of the body/ movements actually being performed’ (Pristas, Pristas and Medak, in Bleeker, 2017, p.118).

2010 Dance Central was released; ‘the first immersive dance video game to feature and track full-body dance moves’ (Harmonix, 2018).

2011 Loops was recreated in cinema-resolution for the New York Film Festival (Kaiser, in Bleeker, 2017).

2011 The first Dance Engaging Science session took place to ‘investigate the current state of interdisciplinary dance science research, and to lay the foundations for future interdisciplinary research in which dance itself plays a major, constituent role’ (MotionBank, 2018).

2011 The Digital Dance Archives were launched, which can be found here.

2013 The Choreographic Coding Lab took place. Read about it on MotionBank's website here.

2014 E-Traces: tracking the electronic traces of devices attached to ballet shoes. Please be sure to click here!


Images from Lesia Trubat's E-Traces (2014). Photography by Luciá González and Marta Guillén.

2015 The Royal Ballet’s The Nutcracker rehearsal was filmed using a 360-degree camera so that viewers can experience the video from “within” the space, and choose interactively where to direct their attention.


2015 body(input), a bio-interactive dance performance by Maria Judova. Find out more here.

2017 Work begins to re-work Loops in Virtual Reality (Kaiser, in Bleeker, 2017).

2018 Virtual Reality: Celestial Bodies is brought to the Sadler’s Wells Sampled festival. The Sampled Extras programme reads: ‘Come and try the latest virtual reality experience from The Guardian, created with Alexander Whitley Dance Company. Inspired by the spectacular imagery of solar physics, Celestial Bodies uses contemporary dance and motion capture technology to explore movement across human and astronomical scales. The user is able to switch between two stunning cosmic worlds and experience choreography in different ways' (Sadler's Wells, 2018).
